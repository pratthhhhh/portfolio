Stereo Camera & Stereo Depth Estimation

A stereo camera system consists of two horizontally offset lenses that mimic human binocular vision. By capturing the same scene from two slightly different viewpoints, the system can infer depth through triangulation, objects closer to the camera appear to shift more between the two images (large disparity), while distant objects shift less (small disparity).

Stereo depth estimation is the computational process of recovering a dense depth map from such stereo image pairs. Modern deep learning approaches, such as the Stereo Transformer (STTR) used in this work, model long-range correspondences along epipolar lines using attention mechanisms, enabling accurate disparity prediction across varying depth ranges — from nearby obstacles to objects over 100 m away.

What I Did — WHT Implementation

Stereo Depth Estimation models are becoming more sophisticated and accurate, but they are also becoming more computationally expensive. These Models are being used in autonomous driving systems, mapping depth and semantin information in real time, where computational efficiency is of utmost importance. The core contribution of this thesis is to make these models more computationally efficient by implementing Hadamard Transform based convolution. In my implementation I replaced the standard Conv2D layers inside the STTR feature extractor with a custom WHTConv2D layer built on the Walsh-Hadamard Transform. The WHT decomposes feature maps using only addition and subtraction operations (no multiplications), drastically reducing FLOPs while retaining comparable representational power.

Additionally, a Log-Disparity Loss formulation is introduced to improve supervision on far-range (>100 m) depth regions — depth ranges that traditional smooth-L1 losses underweight due to their small disparity magnitude. Together, these modifications achieve a 9% reduction in computational complexity with minimal accuracy trade-off.

My Implementation — Results

The modified STTR model was trained and evaluated on the StereoCARLA dataset.
